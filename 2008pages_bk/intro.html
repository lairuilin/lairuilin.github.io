How East Asian classrooms may change over the next 20 years 

Tak-Wai Chan  

 

Chan is Chair Professor of the Graduate Institute of Network Learning Technology at the National Central University of Taiwan. Over the past 25 years, Chan has been working on various subareas of technology enhanced learning, including artificial intelligence in education, computer supported collaborative learning, digital classroom, online learning communities, mobile and ubiquitous learning, digital game based learning, and, more recently, technology enhanced elementary mathematics and language learning.

In the mid-1980s, Chan, then a doctoral student in a computer science department in the USA, studied artificial intelligence. In those days, the main focus of artificial intelligence for applications in learning was based on the assumption that the computer could be as intelligent and effective as a human tutor could be when assisting a student to learn. There were few concerns about learning within a social context. Grounded in Vygotsky's socio-cultural theory of learning, in particular, the zone of proximal development hypothesis, Chan proposed in his doctoral thesis the concept of the learning companion—a learning environment consisting of multiple virtual characters such as a learning companion and a tutor. Learning within such a social context, a student can interact with virtual characters in various social activities such as collaborative learning or competitive games, or the human student and the virtual companion can teach each other. His seminal research on learning companion has now become an active subarea of artificial intelligence in education.

After his PhD was conferred, Chan worked with colleagues to develop a network learning system. They initially connected two IBM compatible personal computers (PCs) via a cable, later extending the system to include many PCs in a computer lab for experiments. In 1992, they published a study describing that work. They called their system a futuristic intelligent classroom. It was probably the first dedicated, synchronous network learning system in the world for collaborative learning and competitive learning games. In the 90s they continued to conduct a series of studies on synchronous network learning taking advantage of connected computers in a classroom setting. In 1999, because laptops were too expensive and personal computers were still too bulky for the classroom, Chan and colleagues developed an inexpensive wireless response clicker system, called EduClick, for enhancing teacher-student interactions in question-and-answer activities. Although realizing later that he was not the first one to conceive of the design of such a clicker, he is surprised by the current popularity of such clickers in classrooms over the world.

In 2000 he led a 4-year learning technology project with a budget of 14 million USD sponsored by the Ministry of Education and National Science Council of Taiwan. The project involved dozens of professors across Taiwan. One subproject was to build the EduCity (www.educities.edu.tw). The EduCity is comprised of a hierarchy of communities: the EduCity itself represents the whole community, consisting of websites of schools called EduTowns; an EduTown represents a school, consisting of the websites of the classes in that school called EduVillages; an EduVillage represents a class, consisting of the personal websites of the students in that class called EduCitizens. Additionally, the EduCity provides students with a large variety of online resources and activities using technology that today we call Web2.0. For example, EduTowns (schools) adopt online application programs called service items provided by the EduCity. They can also develop their own ‘service items' and, if they want, share with other EduTowns. Furthermore, we supported collaboration among teachers, stimulating the development of learning material and lesson plans as open content. Any EduCitizen, who may be a teacher or a student, can open an online course in the EduCity.

Another sub-project was the continuation of the future classroom research. Chan and his colleagues investigated mobile learning, treating the outdoor and the Nature as a part of the future classroom. In actual physical classrooms, they used WebPad, the pen-based and wireless enabled computer, as the platform to develop their system. When Tablet PCs later became available on the market, they switched the devices to Tablet PCs.

When the 4-year project ended in 2003, EduCity had 1.5 million users, possibly the largest online learning community in the world at that time. Its operation was then transferred to the largest telecommunication company in Taiwan to continue.

Chan is currently seeking to develop a conceptual framework and foundation for realizing the ultimate vision of learning companion research—the lifelong learning companion. Besides, continuing his interest in digital classrooms, Chan is investigating a series of social learning games such as reciprocal peer tutoring, learning by asking  good questions, and team competitive learning games such as EduBingo and AnswerMatching in one-to-one classrooms, classrooms in which every student is equipped with a handy computer wirelessly enabled for mediating face-to-face interactions. More recently, he is interested in developing digital content material associated with novel pedagogical models for elementary schools, including mathematics, reading, and writing of Chinese language, as well as learning English as a second language. In addition, for a long time, Chan has been cognizant of a disparity—online learning environments such as EduCity and the learning environments of classrooms have been two separated worlds, no matter how rich the learning resources or how vibrant the communities in EduCity. To address this matter, Chan has been designing a conceptual architecture for the EduCity 2.0.

In a series of keynote speeches delivered at international conferences since the early 2000s, in particular, at AIED2007, Chan has proposed four problems for researchers in the field to resolve in this century. The four problems are the productivity problem, which is about the performance or output/input problem; the school restructuring problem, which is how schools' structure are being transformed; the personalized curriculum problem, which is the extension of intelligent tutoring system research's Holy Grail problem; and the global educational goal problem, which calls for a rethinking of educational goals from the global perspective, demanding a global, collective endeavor.

In 2010, to inspire future digital classroom research, Chan published a position paper in JCAL. There he points out that our schools have been experiencing three overlapping waves of technology adoption since the mid-1980s: the personal computer lab wave, the online learning wave, and the digital classroom wave. He further explains why we are now at the onset of the digital classroom wave, why it will cause great changes in education and why those changes could not occur prior to the digital classroom wave. By proposing 10 conjectures, he describes how classrooms in Taiwan as well as in other East Asian countries may change over the next 20 years.

Besides being one of the earliest researchers in the field in Asia, Chan has a personal mission to help build a regional research community. In the nineties, he co-founded several conference series, ICCEs and GCCCEs, international academic societies, APSCE (www.apsce.net) for the Asia-Pacific region and GCSCE (www.gcsce.org) for global Chinese community. A few years ago, Chan co-founded a network of world-class researchers, G1:1 (www.G1to1.org), for promoting international collaboration in one-to-one technology enhanced learning. Chan has served on the editorial boards of more than ten international journals as well as on project review panels in the US and the EU.

Chan has also been the major designer of the first Master Plan for Information Technology in Education from the Ministry of Education of Taiwan in 2001, the National Science and Technology Program for e-Learning in 2003 (a cross-ministry national program), and the research sub-program of its second phase in 2007.
Although born and raised in Hong Kong, Chan completed his first undergraduate degree in the UK, received his master and doctoral degrees in the USA, and have been working in Taiwan since 1989. So he considers himself a global Chinese. All his family members, other than himself, hold Canadian citizenship. He said he may retire in Africa.
